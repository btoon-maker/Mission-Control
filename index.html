Mission Control: Tracking Aurora-3

This summer, you were selected to join the Mission Control Internship Program at Canyon Observatory.

As a Junior Orbital Response Intern, you will use observatory tools, archived satellite data, and your own observations to help Mission Control track Aurora-3 and figure out what keeps it moving around Earth.

Learning Objectives & Instructional Alignment: In this lesson, students investigate archived and new Aurora-3 orbit data to develop an evidence-based explanation for how Earth’s gravitational pull affects the motion of objects near Earth.

  • Learning Objective 1: Students will be able to obtain and analyze orbit data to identify patterns in how Aurora-3 moves around Earth.
  • Learning Objective 2: Students will be able to use evidence from observations, simulations, and models to explain that Earth’s gravitational force pulls objects inward toward Earth’s center.
  • Learning Objective 3: Students will be able to communicate a recommendation supported by evidence about how Earth’s pull and forward motion affect Aurora-3’s orbit path.
  • Arizona Science Standard: 5.E2U1.8 — Obtain, analyze, and communicate evidence to support an explanation that the gravitational force of Earth on objects is directed toward the planet’s center.
  • Science and Engineering Practices: Analyzing and Interpreting Data; Developing and Using Models; Constructing Explanations; Engaging in Argument from Evidence; Obtaining, Evaluating, and Communicating Information.
  • Crosscutting Concepts: Patterns; Cause and Effect; Systems and System Models; Stability and Change.
  • SINS Framework Alignment: Students enter the lesson through a narrative role as Junior Orbital Response Interns, choose investigation pathways, compare evidence during convergence points, use models for sensemaking, apply their understanding to new orbit data, and export a Mission Journal artifact.

Mission Control: Tracking Aurora-3

Today is your first day as a Junior Orbital Response Intern at Canyon Observatory, where scientists study how satellites move around Earth.

Your mentor, Maya Chen, is part of the orbit tracking team. Her job is to help interns learn how scientists use evidence, models, and observations to understand space systems.

Maya welcomes you and the other interns into Mission Control.

“During this internship, you will not just read about satellites. You will investigate real observation patterns and build your own explanation from the evidence,” Maya says.
“Mission Control may share extra resources along the way. They can help you piece together what is happening with Aurora-3.”
Mission File: Aurora-3

Maya opens the first Aurora-3 mission file on the main Mission Control screen.

“This is Aurora-3,” Maya says. “It is a weather research satellite that travels around Earth collecting information about clouds, storms, and the atmosphere.”

The Mission Control systems track:

  • Aurora-3’s orbital path around Earth
  • changes in position over time
  • movement and direction as it orbits Earth

Your first task is to study the mission data and look for patterns in Aurora-3’s movement.

Maya zooms in on the satellite’s archived orbital path.

“That’s where your investigation begins. Mission Control has prepared several stations to help you piece together what is happening.”
Where will you start?

The Junior Orbital Response Interns gather around the Mission Control observation screens.

“Start with the evidence,” Maya reminds the interns. “Look closely before you try to explain anything.”
“Choose where your team should begin. Each station will give you a different piece of the orbit evidence.”

📖 Mission Glossary
Satellite
An object that moves around a planet or another object in space. Some satellites are made by people to collect information.
Orbit
The path an object follows as it moves around another object in space.
Path
The route an object follows as it moves from place to place.
Pattern
Something that repeats or happens in a way that scientists can observe and compare.
Evidence
Information from observations, data, or models that helps support an explanation.
Model
A representation that helps scientists study, test, or explain how part of a system works.
System
A group of connected parts that work together or affect one another.
Forward Motion
Movement in the direction an object is already traveling.
Recommendation
A suggested decision or action that is supported by evidence.
