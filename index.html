Mission Control: Tracking Aurora-3

This summer, you were selected to join the Mission Control internship program, working alongside scientists and satellite tracking specialists connected to Lowell Observatory.

Your internship mentor, Maya Chen, is part of the orbital tracking team that studies how satellites move around Earth.

As one of the Junior Orbital Response Specialists, your team will help analyze satellite observations, investigate movement patterns, and support ongoing orbital research.

Maya welcomes your team into Mission Control.

"Our teams study how satellites move through space and how they stay in orbit around Earth" Maya explains.
"Today, your team will investigate Aurora-3 and study the patterns scientists use to track orbital movement"
Section 1 of 3
Mission Control Alert

Maya pulls the newest orbital tracking report onto the main screen.

"This is Aurora-3," Maya says. "It's a research satellite designed to study weather patterns and atmospheric changes around Earth."
"Mission Control teams use tracking data like this to study how satellites move around Earth and remain in orbit over time."

Mission Control systems track:

  • Aurora-3’s orbital path around Earth
  • changes in position over time
  • movement and direction during orbit

Scientists study these observations to better understand how satellites travel around Earth and stay in orbit over time.

Maya zooms in on the satellite’s orbital path.

"Your team is going to help analyze the observation data, look for patterns, and investigate how Aurora-3 moves around Earth."

You must decide where to begin analyzing the observation data.

Maya organizes the internship teams around the Mission Control observation screens.

“Each team is studying a different part of Aurora-3’s orbital data,” Maya explains.
"Choose where you would like to begin investigating orbital movement."
Choose ONE investigation path.

Section 3 of 3
Orbit Tracking Team

Maya leads you to the orbital tracking station.

Large screens show Aurora-3 moving around Earth. One path shows where Mission Control predicted the satellite would travel. The other path shows the newest tracking observations.

Maya points to the screen.

"Your job is to compare the predicted orbit with the newest observation data," she explains. "Look closely for patterns. What is staying the same? What seems different?"

You open the tracking system and begin reviewing Aurora-3's orbit data.

🛰️ Use the Tracking System

As you review the data Lowell Observatory received, compare:

  • Predicted Orbit — where Aurora-3 was expected to travel
  • Actual Orbit — the newest tracking observations
  • Both Together — compare the two paths side by side

Use the timeline to observe how Aurora-3 moves at different times.

Remember: your goal is not to find a "right answer" yet. Your goal is to make careful observations and notice patterns.

📓 Journal Prompt — Orbit Tracking Team

Motion Analysis Team

Maya studies the newest tracking visuals carefully.

"The Motion Analysis Team focuses on how the satellite moves and changes direction over time," Maya explains.

You begin reviewing the movement data collected during Aurora-3's recent observation cycles.

🛰️ Use the Motion Analysis System

As you investigate, observe:

  • how Aurora-3 moves during orbit
  • how its direction changes over time
  • how the movement path curves around Earth
  • how the satellite continues traveling forward

Use the timeline and observation tools to track movement patterns across multiple observation points.

Remember: your goal is not to explain the motion yet. Your goal is to carefully observe patterns and collect evidence from the movement data.
