Mission Control: Tracking Aurora-3

This summer, you were selected to join the Mission Control Internship Program at Canyon Observatory, where scientists study how satellites move around Earth.

Your internship mentor, Maya Chen, is part of the orbital tracking team. Her job is to help interns learn how scientists use evidence, models, and observations to understand space systems.

As one of the Junior Orbital Response Specialist interns, you will use archived mission data to investigate how a satellite moves around Earth.

Maya welcomes you and the other interns into Mission Control.

“During this internship, you will not just read about satellites. You will investigate real observation patterns and build your own explanation from the evidence,” Maya says.
“Your final challenge will be to explain what you learned about orbits, satellites, and Earth to someone outside Mission Control.”
Section 1 of 3
Aurora-3 Internship Challenge

Maya opens the internship challenge file on the main Mission Control screen.

“This is Aurora-3,” Maya says. “It is a weather research satellite that travels around Earth collecting information about clouds, storms, and the atmosphere.”

Aurora-3 is already in orbit. Mission Control scientists already understand how satellites function in space. Your job as an intern is to use the observatory’s tools to figure out how the evidence shows those ideas in action.

The Mission Control systems track:

  • Aurora-3’s orbital path around Earth
  • changes in position over time
  • movement and direction during orbit

Your internship challenge is to study the data, notice patterns, and build an explanation for how Aurora-3 stays in orbit around Earth.

Maya zooms in on the satellite’s archived orbital path.

“Mission Control has prepared several investigation stations for you. Each station will help you collect a different piece of evidence.”

Gravity: Earth’s Pull

The intern teams continue reviewing the Mission Control model.

Aurora-3 keeps moving forward while its path continues curving around Earth.

“Your investigations revealed an important pattern,” Maya says.
“Earth continuously pulls on objects nearby, even while those objects continue moving forward through space.”

Maya points toward the curved orbit path displayed on the Mission Control screen.

“Scientists call this pull gravity.”
“Gravity helps explain why Aurora-3’s path curves around Earth instead of continuing in a straight line.”
